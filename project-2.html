PROJECT 02

Dragon

In this project, I wanted to showcase the Chinese dragon in different eras, so I created a one- to two-minute animation that combines it with Chinese history.

OVERVIEW

Project Overview

The representation of dragons in contemporary animations tends to be homogenized, and it is easy to ignore the richness of history and region. At the same time, traditional patterns lack dynamic visual transformation paths in research, and people often ignore their evolution process.

This work explores the evolution of Chinese dragon patterns with the development of human civilization. In the process of making animations, I mainly used ink and wash to depict, because ink and wash is not only one of the important elements in Chinese painting, but also can make the dragon's form more fluent. At the same time, I combined it with history to mobilize the audience's subjective emotions to help people understand better.

I follow the logic from natural elements (gold, wood, water, fire, earth) to cultural generation to construct the overall direction of the animation.

This project is based on how to express the diversity of Chinese dragons in the form of 2D animation and through colors and lines, and how to combine Chinese dragon patterns with specific cultural elements to help people from different cultures better understand the connotation of Chinese dragons.

This provides a new paradigm for cultural digital protection and plays a role in cultural communication by breaking stereotypes.

PROCESS

Process & Development

I started *Dragon* with the goal of challenging the homogenized representation of dragons in contemporary animation. I wanted to showcase how Chinese dragon patterns evolved across different eras, tracing their transformation alongside human civilization. I chose ink-wash as the primary medium because its flowing quality suits the dragon's fluid form. I also structured the animation around the logic of natural elements—gold, wood, water, fire, earth—to guide the visual transition from natural forms to cultural symbols. My aim was to help audiences from different cultures better understand the depth of Chinese dragon imagery.

A significant difficulty was visualizing the evolutionary process without making it feel like a dry historical timeline. Early versions were too linear and lacked emotional engagement. I solved this by using color and line weight to differentiate eras—darker, rougher strokes for early depictions, smoother and more intricate lines for later dynasties. I also added subtle cultural motifs layered into the backgrounds to connect each dragon form to its historical context. From this project, I learned that cultural storytelling benefits from balancing research with expressive visual rhythm. It reinforced my interest in using animation for cultural preservation and cross-cultural communication.

REFLECTION

Process Reflection

Creating Dragon taught me that cultural storytelling requires more than visual accuracy—it demands a careful balance between research and emotional rhythm. Early in the process, I struggled with making the evolution of dragon patterns feel engaging rather than like a historical lecture. My initial cuts were too linear, and the pacing felt flat. I realized that I needed to let the ink-wash style guide the transitions, not just the chronology. By experimenting with brushstroke weight and color saturation—rougher, darker strokes for ancient dragons, smoother and more intricate lines for later dynasties—I was able to weave historical shifts into the visual texture itself. I also added subtle cultural motifs in the backgrounds to anchor each era without interrupting the flow. This project reinforced my belief that animation can serve as a powerful tool for cultural preservation, but only when the visual language is as thoughtfully constructed as the historical content.
