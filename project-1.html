OVERVIEW

Project Overview

The representation of space in animation often relies on Western realistic techniques such as linear perspective and chiaroscuro to create the familiar illusion of three-dimensionality. However, this singular spatial representation limits animation's potential to express abstract emotions and the tension of movement.

My project, an ink-and-wash animation based on classic Peking Opera, explores how to leverage the unique spatial compositional principles of ink painting to express spatiality and enhance the visual tension. Representing a classic Peking Opera night fight scene through ink painting, the project intentionally blurs the boundaries of physical space. The characters’ bodies and surroundings are shaped by the brush and ink, leveraging the ambiguity and uncertainty of the edges to allow the “real” characters and the “virtual” space to interpenetrate and transform into one another.

This project aims to demonstrate how to better transcend the limitations of physical space to achieve imaginative transformation. This perspective further explores the potential value of returning to and reconstructing this ancient painting genre and its spatial concepts in expanding the expressive dimensions of animation art in a world dominated by digital animation technology.

CONCEPT

Concept & Development

My initial goal for *Crossroads* was to explore how spatial perception could be dynamically reconstructed through animation. Inspired by the classic Peking Opera *Three Forks*, I wanted to combine ink-wash painting style with a night fight scene to create a one- to two-minute animation. I chose ink-wash because its fluidity and ambiguous edges allow space to become flexible rather than fixed, offering a departure from Western linear perspective and chiaroscuro. I wanted to see whether this ancient visual language could express emotional tension and movement more freely.

The main challenge was balancing narrative clarity with spatial ambiguity. In early drafts, the fight sequences became visually chaotic—viewers could not follow who was fighting whom. I revised the storyboard by anchoring key beats with clearer character silhouettes while preserving the ink's bleeding edges. I also experimented with brushstroke density to differentiate foreground and background. Through this process, I learned that abstraction in animation requires deliberate rhythm control. This project ultimately showed me that returning to traditional painting techniques can expand the expressive possibilities of digital animation.

REFLECTION

Process Reflection

This project began with a clear visual intention, but its development changed significantly during production. At the beginning, the focus was mainly on mood, presentation, and overall visual coherence. However, through testing and revision, I realised that some of the early ideas were either too broad or too visually heavy, which made the work harder to control.

One of the most important lessons from this project was learning how to balance concept, structure, and visual clarity. Instead of simply adding more elements, I had to refine what was essential and think more carefully about how each image, layout, and design decision contributed to the final reading of the project.
